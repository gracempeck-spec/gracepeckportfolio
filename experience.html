2024 Exhibition — Salon-Style Installation

For my first solo curatorial project, I drew upon my background in art history to develop a distinctive exhibition design inspired by the salon-style installations of the 17th-century French Academy. Rather than organizing works solely by subject matter, I employed a color-driven curatorial approach, carefully considering how individual pieces would visually interact and create dialogue across the gallery space.

Using principles of color theory and composition, I designed each wall as a cohesive visual experience. Larger, dynamic works served as focal points, while smaller pieces were strategically selected and positioned to complement their palettes and composition. The result was a series of interconnected displays, each with its own atmosphere, narrative, and emotional resonance.

The exhibition received significant praise for its thoughtful installation and visual impact, demonstrating my ability to translate art historical concepts into engaging contemporary experiences.

2025 Exhibition — The Community Wall

Building upon the salon-style exhibition I curated the previous year, I sought to refine the installation approach to better serve both artists and visitors. While the fully integrated arrangement successfully encouraged viewers to discover connections between different artists' works, it also made it more challenging for patrons specifically interested in following individual artists.

To address this, I developed a hybrid curatorial model that balanced individual artist representation with the collaborative spirit of the exhibition. I grouped works into small collections of two to four artists whose pieces shared complementary color palettes, themes, or compositional qualities.

To preserve the sense of community that had been central to the exhibition's identity, I also designed a dedicated "Community Wall" featuring a single work from every participating artist. Inspired by the densely hung salon exhibitions of France, the installation celebrated the collective contribution of the artists and visually reinforced the exhibition's shared mission.

The exhibition design was well received by both artists and visitors, who appreciated the balance between individual representation and collective presentation. The project successfully fostered community engagement while contributing to the exhibition's broader fundraising goals.

County Archivist

Washington County, New York

Upon accepting the role of County Archivist, one of my primary objectives was restoring public access to the archives. Although the facility had closed during the COVID-19 pandemic for safety reasons, it had never fully reopened, and significant collection management challenges had emerged during that period. Due to space limitations, portions of the archival collection had expanded beyond secured storage areas into public spaces, creating organizational, accessibility, and preservation concerns.

I began by conducting a comprehensive assessment of the collection and storage environment to develop a more efficient and sustainable organizational system. This included relocating the map collection to a dedicated area that improved both storage capacity and researcher access, installing additional shelving, and strategically moving frequently requested records closer to administrative offices to streamline internal use.

To improve collection stewardship and ensure compliance with records management standards, I reviewed retention schedules and identified materials eligible for disposition according to established policies. I then undertook a large-scale inventory and reorganization project, consolidating related collections, improving intellectual and physical control of materials, and arranging resources by subject and use to enhance discoverability. In addition, I digitized frequently requested documents to increase accessibility for staff and reduce handling of original materials.

Once the collections were reorganized and access systems improved, I focused on reestablishing public engagement with the archives. I reopened the research space to visitors, provided tours highlighting the transformation of the facility, and promoted greater awareness of the archives as a community resource.

Through these efforts, I successfully improved both the physical and digital accessibility of the collection, strengthened records management practices, optimized storage and retrieval systems, and restored public access to an important historical resource.

Museum Educator

Tang Teaching Museum at Skidmore College

In addition to facilitating guided tours for K–12 school groups, I contributed to the development and delivery of public programs at the Tang Teaching Museum through its biweekly Family Saturdays initiative. These programs were designed to connect visitors of all ages with works from the museum's collection through hands-on artmaking and interactive learning experiences.

I was selected to independently develop and lead a Family Saturdays program from concept to implementation. This process began with researching the museum's collection to identify an artwork that would serve as an engaging educational focal point. After selecting a costume design piece created for an experimental art film called "Beehive" — a sculptural hat inspired by bees and the natural world — I conducted research on the artist, the work's creative context, and its thematic connections. Using this research, I developed an interpretive presentation that introduced visitors to the artwork and encouraged discussion about creativity, design, and artistic inspiration.

Building on these themes, I designed an activity that invited participants to create imaginative hats inspired by the featured work. I developed the project plan, identified learning objectives, sourced materials, and coordinated all aspects of program preparation and execution.

The program successfully engaged a diverse audience of children and caregivers, encouraging creative expression and personal connections to the artwork. The activity fostered meaningful family engagement, with several parents choosing to participate alongside their children.

Museum Educator

MASS MoCA — Massachusetts Museum of Contemporary Art

During my time at MASS MoCA, I developed and facilitated an educational art activity for Kidspace inspired by Love, an installation by Anne Samat. The project required translating key themes and artistic processes from the exhibition into an accessible, hands-on experience for visitors of varying ages and backgrounds.

After researching the installation, I identified Samat's use of found and repurposed materials as a central aspect of her artistic practice. Recognizing an opportunity to connect visitors with both the artist's methodology and the exhibition's themes, I designed a collage-based activity using recycled magazines and printed materials. Participants were invited to create visual "love letters" that explored what love meant to them — whether through relationships with family and friends, connections to nature, personal passions, or broader expressions of care and community.

The project was intentionally designed to be open-ended, allowing visitors to engage with the artwork through personal reflection, creative expression, and experimentation with mixed-media techniques. By adapting the artist's approach to upcycling and assemblage into an accessible educational format, I created a meaningful entry point for visitors to connect with contemporary art concepts.

The activity was well received by participants, who engaged with the project in diverse and thoughtful ways. This project demonstrated my ability to develop audience-centered educational programming that bridges contemporary art, interpretation, and creative learning.

Public Programs Intern

The Strong National Museum of Play

During my Public Programming Internship at The Strong National Museum of Play, I worked within the museum's makerspace — an interactive learning environment designed to foster creativity, experimentation, and hands-on engagement for visitors of all ages. As part of this role, I contributed to the development and facilitation of themed educational activities that encouraged visitors to connect with the museum's mission through creative play.

For a week focused on LEGO-themed programming, I was tasked with developing an original activity that would be engaging, accessible, and adaptable to a broad audience. Through the planning process, I evaluated several concepts before shifting my focus toward a more inclusive and open-ended project that could accommodate varying ages, abilities, and levels of experience.

I ultimately developed a "Design and Create a LEGO Minifigure" activity, creating a custom template that allowed visitors to imagine and illustrate their own LEGO-inspired character. The project encouraged self-expression, storytelling, and creative problem-solving while remaining approachable for both children and adults. I designed the activity materials, prepared program resources, and facilitated visitor participation throughout the event.

The program proved highly successful, generating enthusiastic engagement from visitors and families. Many participants expanded the activity beyond a single character, creating entire collections of minifigures representing themselves, family members, and original characters.
